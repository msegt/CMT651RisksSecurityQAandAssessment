Risk Management

Understanding IT governance, risk identification, and the ISO 31000 framework for managing risks in software development projects.

Governance Overview

IT Governance directs IT endeavours to ensure performance meets key objectives:

  • Alignment of IT with enterprise and realization of promised benefits
  • Use of IT to enable the enterprise by exploiting opportunities
  • Responsible use of IT resources
  • Appropriate management of IT-related risks and opportunities

The governance structure operates at multiple levels, as shown in the diagram below, ensuring that strategic decisions cascade down through programmes and projects to delivery.

PORTFOLIO LEVEL:

PROGRAMMES LEVEL:

Manages programmes to deliver business benefits

PROJECTS LEVEL:

  • RUN THE BUSINESS: Operating current systems and processes
  • CHANGE THE BUSINESS: Strategic transformation and improvements

Key Governance Takeaways
  • Strategic governance requires clear structure and accountability
  • All levels (Portfolio, Programmes, Projects) must work together
  • Business benefits are managed at programme level
  • Project outputs contribute to programme benefits
  • Governance ensures alignment between business strategy and delivery

ISO 31000 Framework

ISO 31000 provides principles, guidelines, and processes for effective risk management:

  • Establishes risk management frameworks within organizations
  • Provides systematic processes for identifying and treating risks
  • Integrates risk management into strategic and operational decisions
  • Enables organizations to identify opportunities and threats
  • Supports continual improvement of risk management practices

The framework is universal and applies across any industry or sector, from small businesses to large enterprises.

Risk Management Process

  1. Establish Context: Define the organization's internal and external environment, objectives, and risk criteria
  2. Risk Identification: Systematically identify risks that might affect objectives
  3. Risk Analysis: Analyze identified risks to understand likelihood and potential consequences
  4. Risk Evaluation: Compare risk analysis results against risk criteria to prioritize risks
  5. Risk Treatment: Select and implement strategies to address identified risks
  6. Monitor & Review: Continuously monitor risk environment and effectiveness of controls

ISO 27005 Overview

ISO/IEC 27005 provides guidelines for managing information security risks:

  • Systematic approach to identifying and assessing security risks
  • Integration with ISO 27001 (ISMS) requirements
  • Context establishment and threat identification
  • Risk analysis and evaluation
  • Treatment planning and implementation
  • Monitoring and review cycles

The standard is relationship-based and complements ISO/IEC 27001, providing the risk management framework that organizations use to establish their Information Security Management System.

ISO/IEC 27005 Benefits

KEY BENEFITS OF ISO/IEC 27005

The ISO/IEC 27005 standard provides organizations with:

INFORMATION SECURITY MANAGEMENT

Systematic approach to managing information security risks and ensuring information protection through structured processes.

VULNERABILITY & THREAT MANAGEMENT

Structured methods for systematically identifying, assessing, and addressing security weaknesses and emerging threats.

PROPORTIONATE CONTROLS

Balanced approach to risk identification and control implementation, ensuring organizations invest effort proportionately to risk levels.

ASSESSMENT FLEXIBILITY

Two complementary approaches to risk assessment:

  • Event-based approach: Focuses on specific threatening events and their consequences
  • Asset-based approach: Focuses on protection of valuable organizational assets

ISO 27001 COMPLIANCE

Direct support for organizations implementing ISO/IEC 27001 (Information Security Management System) requirements regarding risk management.

Key Takeaways
  • ISO 27005 is the risk management component of ISO 27001
  • Provides systematic processes for security risk management
  • Balances control effectiveness with implementation effort
  • Supports both event-based and asset-based assessment approaches
  • Essential for compliance with ISO 27001 ISMS requirements

How ISO 31000 and ISO 27005 Work Together
ISO 31000: The Parent Framework ‚ñº
  • Sets overall risk management principles and structure
  • Defines the general risk management process
  • Applicable to all types of organizational risks
  • Provides governance structure for risk management
  • Ensures consistent risk approach across organization
ISO 27005: The Specialized Application ‚ñº
  • Applies ISO 31000 principles specifically to information security
  • Focuses on security risks, vulnerabilities, and threats
  • Part of the ISO/IEC 27000 family for ISMS
  • Provides deeper technical guidance on security risk assessment
  • Offers specialized tools and techniques for security context
Integration Approach ‚ñº
  • Organizations implement ISO 31000 as enterprise framework
  • Use ISO 27005 for information security risk management within that framework
  • ISO 27005 risk management feeds into overall ISO 31000 risk register
  • Information security risks identified via ISO 27005 are part of enterprise risks under ISO 31000
  • Coordinated approach ensures no security risks are overlooked
Typical Organization Structure ‚ñº
  • Risk Management Team: Manages ISO 31000 enterprise risk framework
  • Information Security Team: Manages ISO 27005 security-specific risks
  • Both report to Risk Committee or Executive Board
  • Coordinated approach ensures comprehensive risk management
  • Regular communication between teams prevents gaps
When to Use Each Standard

Use ISO 31000 When:

  • You need an enterprise-wide risk management framework
  • Managing multiple types of organizational risks
  • Creating an organization-wide risk register
  • Coordinating risk management across departments
  • Aligning risk management with business strategy
  • Meeting governance requirements at board level

Use ISO 27005 When:

  • Establishing information security management system (ISO 27001)
  • Assessing information security risks specifically
  • Conducting security threat and vulnerability assessments
  • Implementing security controls for data protection
  • Meeting cybersecurity compliance requirements
  • Conducting security risk audits

Use Both Together When:

  • Implementing comprehensive organizational governance
  • Ensuring information security risks are integrated with enterprise risks
  • Meeting both general and security-specific compliance requirements
  • Creating holistic risk management and security programs
  • Developing Board-level risk dashboards including security risks
  • Meeting regulatory requirements across multiple domains
Real-World Example: E-Commerce Platform Development

Scenario: A software development company is building a new e-commerce platform for a client.

ISO 31000 IDENTIFIES ALL ORGANIZATIONAL RISKS:

  • Project risks: Schedule delays, resource constraints, technical complexity
  • Business risks: Market changes, competitive threats, partnership issues
  • Operational risks: Process failures, quality issues, supplier disruption
  • Financial risks: Budget overruns, cost overruns, ROI concerns
  • Reputational risks: Brand damage, customer dissatisfaction
  • INFORMATION SECURITY RISKS (as one category among many)

ISO 27005 SPECIFICALLY MANAGES SECURITY RISKS:

  • Customer data breaches exposing personal information
  • Unauthorized access to payment processing systems
  • SQL injection attacks compromising the database
  • Privacy violations affecting user data
  • Authentication and encryption failures
  • Man-in-the-middle attacks on data transmission

HOW THEY WORK TOGETHER:

  1. ISO 31000 identifies information security as a risk category
  2. ISO 27005 provides detailed security risk assessment
  3. Specific security risks (from ISO 27005) are documented in ISO 31000 risk register
  4. Risk priorities are established considering both business impact and security criticality
  5. Security controls (from ISO 27005) are implemented as risk treatments
  6. Overall organizational risk is monitored at ISO 31000 level
  7. Information security risks are continuously monitored at ISO 27005 level
Key Integration Benefits
  • Comprehensive risk coverage across all organizational domains
  • Security risks receive appropriate priority within broader business context
  • Specialized security expertise applied where needed
  • Executive leadership sees complete risk picture
  • Coordinated risk treatments optimize resource allocation

OWASP Top 10 Vulnerabilities

The Open Web Application Security Project (OWASP) maintains a regularly updated list of the most critical web application security risks.

A01 - Broken Access Control CRITICAL ‚ñº

Description: Users can perform unauthorized actions or access restricted data

Examples:

  • Admin functionality accessible without authentication check
  • Viewing or editing someone else's account
  • Insecure direct object references (IDOR)

Mitigation:

  • Implement proper authentication mechanisms
  • Use role-based access control (RBAC)
  • Follow principle of deny by default
  • Audit access logs regularly
A02 - Cryptographic Failures CRITICAL ‚ñº

Description: Sensitive data exposed due to weak encryption or poor cryptographic practices

Examples:

  • Passwords stored in plain text
  • Transmitting data over HTTP instead of HTTPS
  • Using weak cryptographic algorithms

Mitigation:

  • Use strong encryption algorithms (AES-256)
  • Hash passwords with salt (bcrypt, scrypt)
  • Encrypt data in transit using HTTPS/TLS
  • Manage cryptographic keys securely
A03 - Injection CRITICAL ‚ñº

Description: Untrusted data is interpreted as code or commands

Example SQL Injection:

SELECT * FROM users WHERE id = 1 OR 1=1

Mitigation:

  • Use parameterized queries and prepared statements
  • Input validation and sanitization
  • Principle of least privilege for database access
  • Web application firewalls
A04 - Insecure Design HIGH ‚ñº

Description: Missing security controls and threat modeling in design phase

Examples:

  • No rate limiting on login attempts
  • Business logic flaws
  • Missing security requirements

Mitigation:

  • Threat modeling during design phase
  • Security requirements in user stories
  • Security design reviews
A05 - Security Misconfiguration HIGH ‚ñº

Description: Default credentials, debugging enabled, unnecessary features, insecure settings

Examples:

  • AWS bucket with default public permissions
  • Debugging features enabled in production
  • Default credentials still in use

Mitigation:

  • Disable unnecessary default accounts
  • Remove or disable unnecessary features
  • Use security scanning tools
A06 - Vulnerable and Outdated Components HIGH ‚ñº

Description: Using components with known vulnerabilities or outdated versions

Mitigation:

  • Regular dependency scanning
  • Automated update processes
  • Remove unused dependencies
  • Subscribe to security advisories
A07 - Identification and Authentication Failures HIGH ‚ñº

Description: Weaknesses in authentication and session management

Examples:

  • Weak password policies
  • Exposed session IDs in URLs
  • Flawed multi-factor authentication

Mitigation:

  • Implement MFA
  • Enforce strong password policies
  • Secure session management
A08 - Software and Data Integrity Failures HIGH ‚ñº

Description: Code and infrastructure without integrity verification

Mitigation:

  • Digital signatures for updates
  • Verify integrity of dependencies
  • Secure CI/CD configuration
A09 - Security Logging and Monitoring Failures MEDIUM ‚ñº

Description: Insufficient logging allowing attacks to go undetected

Mitigation:

  • Comprehensive logging strategy
  • Centralized log management
  • Real-time monitoring
  • Automated alerts
A10 - Server-Side Request Forgery (SSRF) MEDIUM ‚ñº

Description: Attackers abuse server functionality to access internal resources

Mitigation:

  • Input validation of URLs
  • Network segmentation
  • Deny-by-default firewall rules

Quality Assurance

Agile QA practices, testing methodologies, continuous integration, and code review best practices for delivering high-quality software.

Agile QA Principles

  • Testing Early and Often - Begin testing from the first sprint
  • Frequent Feedback Loops - Rapid iteration and validation
  • Cross-Functional Collaboration - Developers, testers, and stakeholders work together
  • Shift-Left Testing - Move testing earlier in the development lifecycle
  • Continuous Improvement - Regular retrospectives and process refinement
  • Automated Testing - Reduce manual effort and increase coverage
Shift-Left Testing

The shift-left approach moves testing activities earlier in the software development lifecycle. This enables early defect detection when fixes are cheaper and faster, reduces rework costs, and improves overall product quality.

INVEST Checklist - Definition of Ready

User stories should meet the INVEST criteria before being brought into a sprint.

I

Independent

Stories should have no dependencies on other stories, allowing them to be developed in any order.

Benefit: Flexibility in prioritization

N

Negotiable

Stories should prompt discussion without prescribing the exact solution.

Benefit: Encourages team creativity

V

Valuable

Each story must deliver clear value to a specific user or stakeholder.

Benefit: Ensures work aligns with user needs

E

Estimable

The team must be able to estimate the story's size with reasonable confidence.

Benefit: Enables sprint planning

S

Small

Stories should represent the smallest piece that delivers useful, working software.

Benefit: Faster feedback, reduced risk

T

Testable

Clear acceptance criteria must exist to verify the story is complete.

Benefit: Objective completion criteria

Definition of Done

A checklist of activities that must be completed before a user story or task is considered "done."

Development DoD
  • Working code checked into version control
  • Unit tests written and passing
  • Code reviewed and meets quality standards
  • All bugs and exceptions fixed and tested
  • Acceptance criteria met and verified
  • Technical documentation updated
  • No regression in existing functionality
Testing DoD
  • Test cases created (manual/automated/hybrid)
  • Positive and negative test scenarios covered
  • Regression tests included and passing
  • Tests reviewed and updated in test suite
  • All identified bugs confirmed fixed
  • Performance and security considerations addressed
  • Accessibility requirements validated

Testing Types in Agile

Unit Testing ‚ñº

Purpose: Test individual components or functions in isolation

Characteristics:

  • Fast execution (milliseconds)
  • High code coverage targets (70-90%)
  • Automated and run frequently
  • Written by developers

Tools: JUnit, Pytest, Jest, NUnit

Integration Testing ‚ñº

Purpose: Verify that different components work together correctly

Characteristics:

  • Tests component interactions and interfaces
  • May involve databases, APIs, or external services
  • Slower than unit tests
  • Can be automated or manual

Tools: Selenium, Postman, REST Assured

System Testing ‚ñº

Purpose: Validate end-to-end scenarios from a user perspective

Characteristics:

  • Tests complete user workflows
  • Performed in environment similar to production
  • Includes functional and non-functional testing
  • May involve UI automation

Tools: Selenium, Cucumber, TestComplete

Regression Testing ‚ñº

Purpose: Ensure new changes haven't broken existing functionality

Characteristics:

  • Re-runs existing test suites
  • Highly automated for efficiency
  • Critical for continuous delivery
  • Grows over time as features are added
Acceptance Testing ‚ñº

Purpose: Verify that user story acceptance criteria are met

Characteristics:

  • Based on user story acceptance criteria
  • Often performed by product owner or QA
  • Can be automated (BDD frameworks)
  • Final validation before marking story as done

Code Review Best Practices

Reviewer Responsibilities
  • Understand why the change is necessary
  • Review for simplicity and clarity
  • Identify alternative approaches
  • Check adherence to coding standards
  • Verify tests are adequate
  • Provide constructive feedback
  • Track review iterations
Author Responsibilities
  • Explain reasoning behind changes
  • Keep changes focused and atomic
  • Respond to all feedback thoughtfully
  • Extract unrelated changes
  • Update documentation as needed
  • Ensure all tests pass before review
Merge Request Evaluation Criteria
  • Does it solve the stated problem?
  • Is the code readable and maintainable?
  • Are there adequate tests with good coverage?
  • Does it follow project conventions?
  • Are there any security or performance concerns?
  • Is the Definition of Done satisfied?

Continuous Integration & Deployment (CI/CD)

CI/CD automates the software delivery pipeline from code commit to production deployment.

Key Components:

  • Version Control System: Central repository for code (GitHub, GitLab)
  • Automated Build: Compile code and create artifacts
  • Automated Testing: Unit, integration, and regression tests
  • Code Quality Checks: Linting, static analysis, security scanning
  • Artifact Management: Store build artifacts
  • Deployment Automation: Deploy to staging and production
  • Monitoring: Track application health and performance

Benefits:

  • Faster feedback loops on code quality
  • Enhanced collaboration between developers
  • Increased test coverage and quality
  • Reduced manual testing effort
  • More frequent, reliable releases
  • Faster time to market

Legal & Ethical Considerations

Understanding legal obligations, regulatory compliance, and professional ethics in software development.

Computer Misuse Act 1990 (UK)

The Computer Misuse Act is the primary UK legislation addressing cybercrime and unauthorized access to computer systems.

Section Offense Maximum Penalty
Section 1 Unauthorized access to computer material 2 years imprisonment and/or fine
Section 2 Unauthorized access with intent to commit further offences 5 years imprisonment and/or fine
Section 3 Unauthorized acts with intent to impair computer operation 10 years imprisonment and/or fine
Section 3ZA Unauthorized acts causing serious damage 14 years (life if national security risk)
Implications for Developers

Be aware of legal boundaries when:

  • Testing security vulnerabilities (always get written authorization)
  • Accessing systems or data (only access what you're authorized for)
  • Developing security tools (ensure legitimate use cases)
  • Handling user data (respect access controls and privacy)

GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)

The GDPR is EU regulation that applies to any organization processing personal data of EU residents.

Key Obligations for Software Developers:

  • Data Protection by Design and Default: Build privacy into systems from the start
  • Appropriate Technical Safeguards: Encryption, access controls, secure coding
  • Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability: Ensure CIA triad for personal data
  • Resilience: Systems can recover from incidents
  • Risk Assessment: Evaluate and mitigate data processing risks
  • Avoid Known Vulnerabilities: Keep systems patched and secure

Data Subject Rights:

  • Right to access their data
  • Right to rectification (correction)
  • Right to erasure ("right to be forgotten")
  • Right to data portability
  • Right to object to processing
Penalties for Non-Compliance

Fines up to ‚Ç¨20 million or 4% of global annual revenue, whichever is higher, for serious violations.

Intellectual Property

Copyright

Protects original works including source code, documentation, and designs.

  • Automatic protection upon creation
  • Employer typically owns work created by employees
  • Freelance work requires explicit assignment
Patents

Protect inventions and novel processes.

  • Must be novel, non-obvious, and useful
  • Software patents controversial in many jurisdictions
  • Requires formal application and examination
Trademarks

Protect brand names, logos, and distinctive marks.

  • Prevents confusion in marketplace
  • Requires registration for full protection
  • Must be actively defended
Software Licensing

Governs how software can be used and distributed.

  • Proprietary: Restrictive, closed-source
  • Open Source: Various licenses (MIT, GPL, Apache)
  • Compliance: Must respect license terms

Professional Ethics

  • Avoid misuse of employer resources

  • Act as a role model for ethical behavior
